# Engineered Parquet: The Complete Buyer's Guide 2026

*Last updated: 13 April 2026*

Renovating your home and trying to choose the right floor — but the more you read, the more confusing it gets? Engineered parquet, solid hardwood, engineered hardwood, laminate — the terminology keeps shifting, and the differences aren't always clear.

Engineered parquet is real wood, cleverly engineered: a natural oak wear layer bonded to a stable multi-layer base. The result is a floor that looks and feels like solid hardwood, but handles underfloor heating, seasonal humidity changes, and daily life considerably better.

This guide covers everything — from construction and thickness to room suitability, finish options, and why the origin of the wood matters more than most buyers realise.

> **Key Takeaways:**
> - Engineered parquet is real wood — a natural oak wear layer bonded to a stable multi-layer core (HDF or plywood).
> - It can be installed over underfloor heating at a maximum surface temperature of 27°C.
> - The thickness of the wear layer determines how many times the floor can be sanded and refinished.
> - Suitable for living rooms, bedrooms, and hallways — with care in kitchens; not suitable for wet bathrooms.
> - Our collection features European oak from Ter Hürne (Germany) and DIG (Netherlands) — certified quality with documented provenance.

## What is engineered parquet and how is it made? {#kakvo-e}

Engineered parquet is a multi-layer wood flooring where a natural oak wear layer is bonded to a core of HDF (High-Density Fibreboard) or plywood. The layers are arranged in a cross-grain pattern at 90-degree angles — and it's precisely this construction that gives the floor its stability.

You may also see it referred to as **engineered hardwood** or **engineered wood flooring** — these all describe the same product. Different retailers use different terms, but the construction is identical: real wood on top, engineered stability underneath.

The cross-grain layers work against the natural tendency of wood to expand and contract with temperature and humidity changes. Solid hardwood — cut from a single piece of timber — moves considerably more and is much more sensitive to heating systems and seasonal climate shifts. Engineered parquet keeps the beauty of real wood with far greater dimensional stability.

## Advantages over other flooring types {#predimstva}

Engineered parquet strikes a balance between the warmth of solid hardwood and the practicality of modern flooring — which is why it's become the most popular choice for new builds and renovations across Europe.

**Versus solid hardwood**

Solid hardwood is beautiful but demanding. Over underfloor heating, or with seasonal humidity swings, it expands and contracts significantly — which over time can lead to gaps, cupping, or cracked boards. Engineered parquet handles these changes far better thanks to its layered construction. It also comes in a wider range of formats and is generally more accessible in price.

**Versus laminate**

Laminate contains no real wood — it's an HDF board with a photographic print layer that mimics timber. Engineered parquet has a genuine oak wear layer, which means a different feel underfoot, a different sound when you walk on it, and an entirely different visual depth. Most importantly: engineered parquet can be sanded and refinished 2–3 times over its lifetime. Laminate cannot.

## How to choose: thickness, grade, and finish {#izbor}

The right engineered parquet comes down to three core parameters. Understand these and the decision becomes much simpler.

### Thickness

Overall plank thickness ranges from 12 to 22 mm. More important, however, is the thickness of the wear layer (the top oak veneer) — typically between 3 and 6 mm. A thicker wear layer allows more sanding cycles if you ever want to refinish the floor. If you're planning to stay in your home for decades, aim for a minimum 4 mm wear layer.

### Grade

The grade describes the visual character of the oak — not its structural quality. Every grade is "right" for a different aesthetic:

- **Prime** — minimal knots, even grain and colour. Clean, contemporary look.
- **Select** — occasional small knots, natural variation. A balance of elegance and character.
- **Rustic (ABC)** — pronounced knots, natural fissures, wild grain. Warm, authentic, full of character.

None of these grades is better than another — it's entirely a matter of the style you're going for.

### Finish: UV lacquer or oil?

**UV lacquer** forms a hard, transparent barrier on the surface — durable, easy to clean, requires no periodic reapplication. **Oil** penetrates deep into the oak's pores, preserves its natural matte appearance, and allows spot repairs when scratches occur — but needs refreshing once every one to two years. Neither is superior; it depends on how you live in your home.

## Underfloor heating and room suitability {#pomeshteniya}

Engineered parquet is compatible with underfloor heating — provided certain conditions are met.

### Underfloor heating

The maximum surface temperature must not exceed 27°C. The thermal resistance of engineered parquet is 0.11–0.14 m²K/W depending on thickness — well within the recommended range for underfloor heating systems. For installation over underfloor heating, we recommend **glue-down installation** rather than floating — a glued floor transfers heat more evenly and doesn't shift with temperature cycles.

## Installation — two methods {#montazh}

Engineered parquet can be installed in two ways — the right choice depends on your room and heating setup.

### Floating installation (click system)

The planks lock together using a click system, with no adhesive required. Installation is fast, DIY-friendly, and reversible if needed. Recommended for rooms without underfloor heating. Always leave an expansion gap of 8–10 mm around the perimeter walls — wood needs room to move naturally with seasonal changes.

### Glue-down installation

The parquet is bonded directly to the prepared subfloor using parquet adhesive. More involved and best left to professionals — but strongly recommended for underfloor heating and wider planks. A glued floor is more stable and conducts heat more effectively.

### Acclimatisation — a step you cannot skip

Before installation, leave the planks in the room for **48–72 hours**. The wood needs to adjust to the room's temperature and humidity — skip this and you risk warping or gapping after installation. Ideal conditions: 18–20°C and 45–65% relative humidity.

## Maintenance and longevity {#poddrajka}

With proper care, engineered parquet lasts **20–30 years**. The rules are straightforward.

### Daily care

Sweep regularly or vacuum with a soft brush attachment — fine grit and particles are the main enemy of any parquet surface. For washing, use a damp (not wet) mop with a cleaner specifically formulated for wood floors. Never let water sit on the surface.

### Lacquer vs oil — the maintenance difference

Lacquered surfaces need only a standard wood floor cleaner and no additional treatment. Oiled surfaces require periodic refreshing with a maintenance oil or wax — typically once a year. When an oiled floor gets scratched, you can repair just the affected area without sanding the entire room — a meaningful advantage over lacquer.

### Sanding and refinishing

Engineered parquet with a wear layer of 4 mm or more can be sanded 2–3 times over its lifetime, effectively resetting visible wear and giving the floor a completely fresh appearance. Laminate and vinyl flooring don't offer this — once worn, the only option is replacement.

### Preventive measures

Use felt pads under furniture legs. Clean up spilled liquids immediately. Avoid dragging heavy objects across the floor.

## European quality — why origin matters {#evropeyski}

Not all engineered parquet is equal — and where the oak comes from is one of the most important factors in long-term quality.

European oak grows slowly — in the forests of France, Germany, and Eastern Europe, trees mature over decades. Slower growth means denser grain, harder timber, and greater resistance to everyday wear. Asian oak grows considerably faster — softer wood, shorter lifespan.

Manufacturing quality matters just as much. European producers with decades of tradition invest in precision adhesives, tight joint tolerances, and specific hand-finishing techniques — brushing, bevelling, smoking — to achieve an authentic appearance. Budget Asian products rarely reach this level of precision, and poor adhesive bonding can lead to delamination after years of use.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What is engineered parquet and how does it differ from solid hardwood?

Engineered parquet is a multi-layer floor with a natural oak wear layer bonded to an HDF or plywood core. Solid hardwood is cut from a single piece of timber — more sensitive to humidity and temperature changes. Engineered parquet preserves the beauty of real wood while offering greater stability, better compatibility with underfloor heating, and easier installation.

### Can engineered parquet be used with underfloor heating?

Yes — engineered parquet is compatible with underfloor heating. The maximum surface temperature must not exceed 27°C. We recommend glue-down installation (rather than floating) for better heat transfer and long-term stability of the floor.

### Can engineered parquet be installed in a kitchen?

Yes, with care. Engineered parquet works well in kitchens as long as you avoid direct contact with water — keep it away from the area immediately beside the sink or dishwasher. A UV lacquer finish is recommended for added protection. For wet zones such as bathrooms, choose Hywood hybrid flooring or SPC flooring instead.

### Oil or lacquer — which finish should I choose?

UV lacquer is more durable and needs minimal upkeep — easy to clean, no periodic reapplication required. Oil preserves the natural matte look of the oak and allows spot repairs when scratches occur, but needs refreshing once a year. The choice comes down to your lifestyle: if you want low maintenance, go lacquer; if you value a natural appearance and the ability to repair locally, go oil.

### Can engineered parquet be sanded and refinished?

Yes — provided the wear layer is thick enough. Parquet with a 4–6 mm wear layer can be sanded 2–3 times over its life, completely renewing the appearance of the floor. Laminate and vinyl flooring cannot be sanded — when they wear out, they need replacing entirely.
